1. Introduction of the Text




Ecclesiastes is a book of the bible in the Old Testament that’s often called “wisdom literature”, along with books like Proverbs and Job. These books tend to be given by a “sage” that often reflects on the nature of the world, virtue, and the place of humans on earth. In Ecclesiastes, the author (“the preacher”) gives wisdom that’s presented as experimental, or experience that the author has had himself and from an observation of the world.
The Epic of Gilgamesh is an epic poem from ancient Mesopotamia, and can be broken down into the Sumerian poems of Bilgamesh and the later Standard Babylonian version (Akkadian version) of Gilgamesh. This poem is in a narrative form, telling the adventures of Gilgamesh, king of Uruk, on a quest against the Guardian Humbaba and to seek immortality and eternal life.




2. Siduri's Wisdom vs. Ecclesiastes 9




In the Epic of Gilgamesh, Gilgamesh goes and seeks out Sinduri - a wise figure - for advice and wisdom on his fate. She tells Gilgamesh that only the gods live forever, and offers advice to embrace mortality in men and instead, enjoy himself in the days of his life. We can see the lines of text below:


You, Gilgames, let your belly be full,

keep enjoying yourself, day and night!
Every day make merry,
dance and play day and night!
Let your clothes be clean!

Let your head be washed, may you be bathed in water!
Gaze on the little one who holds your hand!
Let a wife enjoy your repeated embrace!
Such is the destiny [of mortal men,]

that one who lives [.....]
In the book of Ecclesiastes, the Preacher (narrator) instructs the reader to realize true wisdom - that many things in life are fleeting, a vapor in the wind - and instead, enjoy their days in life and embrace mortality (similar to what is repeated in Gilgamesh). We can see the lines of text below:


Go, eat your bread in gladness, and drink your wine in joy;
for your action was long ago approved by God.


Let your clothes always be freshly washed,
and your head never lack ointment.

Enjoy happiness with a woman you love
all the fleeting days of life that have been granted to you under the sun - all your fleeting days.
For that alone is what you can get out of life

and out of the means you acquire under the sun.




Essentially, both passages talk about how because we all succumb to death, what’s left of humans is to enjoy their toil while they are alive. For Gilgamesh, this is told by Sinduri, a wise figure, while in Ecclesiastes, this seems to be the result of what true wisdom is.

We can see commonalities between the two (that are bolded above). Both assert that because humans should enjoy life such as through an enjoyment to eat, drinking wine gladly (v. 7), wearing clean clothes (v. 8), and being with the women you love (v. 9).
It’s likely that these were common sayings during the time (“enjoyment to eat, wear clean clothes, be with the women you love”) instead of one text copying the other. From an academic standpoint, these were sets of sayings that people had heard, or a shared tradition.




3. Death and the Futility of Life




We see common themes in Gilgamesh and Ecclesiastes, especially in the topic of the acceptance of death and with the futility of life. From an academic viewpoint, we can see that these were common sayings (such as “enjoyment to eat, wear clean clothes, be with the women you love”) during that time period; a set of sayings that people heard, a shared tradition.
The Epic of Gilgamesh takes a more narrative approach to these points of wisdom, through telling the story of the hero Gilgamesh and his legendary exploits. Contrary, we see that in Ecclesiastes, the book teaches of these wisdoms less through a narrative but through an observation of the surrounding world, a reflection of the preacher’s experiences through life.
In Ecclesiastes, we see the theme of death: that death is inevitable, the one event that every living thing ends up to, and happens across class and success. Thus, because we all succumb to death, what’s left of humans is to enjoy their toil while they are alive.

Secondly, we see “futility” (hevel, or fleeting like vapor): the inability of humans to make sense of the world around them. Humans tend to try to make a plan for lives, seek after rewards, and invest in pursuits. But the mindset of the good being rewarded and the wicked being punished doesn’t seem to work in this life, based on the wisdom of the preacher.

We see similar themes in Gilgamesh. Gilgamesh deals with the fact that death is inevitable and that immorality isn’t worth chasing. For example, after Enkidu’s death, Gilgamesh comes to terms with the idea of death, and thus searches for immortality in the denial of death.

But at the end of this journey, Utanapishitm teaches him that immortality is futile and death is inescapable through a simple test: whether he can go days without sleep. Gilgamesh fails, and finally realizes his mortality and accepts death.
